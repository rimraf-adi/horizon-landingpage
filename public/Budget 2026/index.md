# Union Budget 2026–27: A Structured Breakdown


Union Budget 2026–27 positions India on a path of high public investment, gradual fiscal consolidation, and targeted support to key sectors.  
This article walks through the main numbers, policy themes, and sectoral priorities in a clean, professional format.


## Section 1: Macro Picture and Key Numbers


The budget continues the strategy of using high capital expenditure to drive growth while steadily reducing the fiscal deficit.

- Total expenditure (BE 2026–27): ₹53.5 lakh crore.  
- Non‑debt receipts (BE): ₹36.5 lakh crore.  
- Net tax revenue to Centre (BE): ₹28.7 lakh crore.  
- Capital expenditure (public capex): ₹12.2 lakh crore (up from ₹11.2 lakh crore in the current year, and roughly six times the FY15 level).  
- Fiscal deficit (BE 2026–27): 4.3% of GDP; revised estimate for 2025–26: 4.4% of GDP.  
- General government debt‑to‑GDP ratio projected at 55.6% in 2026–27, with a stated glide path towards around 50% by 2030–31.  

The macro message is clear: maintain growth at around 7% with strong public investment, while committing to medium‑term fiscal discipline.


## Section 2: Public Investment and Sectoral Allocations


Public capex is the main instrument of the growth strategy, with transport and infrastructure getting the largest shares.

### Transport, Defence, and Core Sectors


According to portfolio‑wise allocations, transport and defence are the two largest expenditure heads.

- Transport (including roads and railways): ₹5,98,520 crore.  
- Defence: ₹5,94,585 crore.  
- Rural development: ₹2,73,108 crore.  
- Home affairs: ₹2,55,234 crore.  
- Agriculture and allied activities: ₹1,62,671 crore.  
- Education: ₹1,39,289 crore.  
- Energy: ₹1,09,029 crore.  
- Health: ₹1,04,599 crore.  
- Urban development: ₹85,522 crore.  
- IT and telecom: ₹74,560 crore.  

Within these broad heads, some specific line items highlighted include:

- Ministry of Road Transport and Highways: around ₹3.10 lakh crore, mostly for capital expenditure and continued highway expansion.  
- Ministry of Railways: around ₹2.77–2.81 lakh crore, primarily for network expansion, station redevelopment, and rolling stock.  

These allocations reinforce the emphasis on physical connectivity as a driver of productivity and employment.


### Manufacturing and “Make in India” Schemes


The budget lists several schemes targeted at building domestic manufacturing capacity in strategic areas.

Key programmes include:

- Semiconductor ecosystem support (under an expanded ISM framework).  
- Biopharma SHAKTI for high‑end pharmaceutical and biotech capabilities.  
- Electronics Components Manufacturing Scheme.  
- Integrated Programme for Textiles.  
- Three dedicated chemical parks and a container manufacturing scheme.  
- An advanced construction and infrastructure equipment initiative, including revival of 200 legacy industrial clusters.  

The combined intent is to reduce critical import dependencies and deepen domestic value chains.


## Section 3: Fiscal Discipline and Revenue Measures


The budget reiterates the commitment to fiscal consolidation, using both expenditure control and calibrated revenue measures.

### Fiscal Path and Borrowing


- Fiscal deficit: 4.4% of GDP in RE 2025–26 and 4.3% of GDP in BE 2026–27, meeting the earlier commitment to bring the deficit below 4.5% by 2025–26.  
- Non‑debt receipts and expenditure figures imply net market borrowings from dated securities of about ₹11.7 lakh crore, with the balance financed through small savings and other sources.  

This consolidating path is positioned as the operational anchor for the medium‑term debt‑to‑GDP target.


### Direct Tax Measures (High‑Level)


The detailed Finance Bill text runs into several clauses, but the main direct‑tax changes listed include:

- Reduction in Tax Collected at Source (TCS) on the sale of overseas tour program packages from 5% and 20% to a uniform 2%, without any threshold linked to amount.  
- Reduction in TCS rate for remittances under the Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS) for education and medical treatment from 5% to 2%.  
- Tax Deducted at Source (TDS) on supply of manpower services rationalised to 1% or 2% depending on the nature of arrangement.  
- Various rationalisation measures relating to non‑resident taxation, presumptive regimes, and minimum alternate tax (MAT) exemptions for certain non‑residents.  

Income‑tax slab rates themselves are not changed; the focus is instead on simplification and rationalisation of procedures and special provisions.


## Section 4: People‑Centric Development and Social Sectors


The budget places repeated emphasis on “people‑centric development”, particularly in health, social protection, and agriculture.

### Health, Care Economy, and Disability Support


The social sector emphasis spans mental health, geriatric care, and disability support:

- Establishment of a NIMHANS‑2 and upgradation of National Mental Health Institutes in Ranchi and Tezpur to strengthen mental‑health infrastructure.  
- Setting up of emergency and trauma care centres in district hospitals to improve response to accidents and medical emergencies.  
- Creation of a “care economy” through training of 1.5 lakh multiskilled caregivers in geriatric and allied care services.  
- Scaling up of assistive device production, investment in R&D including AI applications, and strengthening of PM Divyasha Kendras as modern retail‑style centres for persons with disabilities.  

These measures sit alongside the broader health allocation for 2026–27, with health and family welfare receiving a little over ₹1 lakh crore.


### Agriculture, Rural Development, and Livelihoods


Agriculture and allied activities, and rural development, together account for a significant share of total expenditure.

- Agriculture and allied activities: ₹1,62,671 crore.  
- Rural development: ₹2,73,108 crore.  

Within this, the official documents highlight:

- Focus on high‑value agriculture, livestock, and fisheries to diversify rural incomes.  
- Greater emphasis on storage, irrigation, and water‑resource projects, reflected in reservoir and rural infrastructure initiatives.  
- Support for self‑help groups and “Self‑Help Entrepreneur (SHE) Marts” as community owned retail outlets at cluster‑level federations.  

The intent is to move from subsistence‑oriented support towards productivity, diversification, and market integration for rural households.


## Section 5: Trade Facilitation, Customs, and Digital Governance


The budget uses customs, logistics reforms, and digitisation to improve ease of doing business and reduce transaction costs for trade.

### Customs and Trade Facilitation


Key customs‑related proposals include:

- Rationalisation of customs duty exemptions for specialised inputs used in sectors like aircraft maintenance, defence, electronics, and select export‑oriented industries.  
- Measures to ease compliance for “trusted importers”, including extended duty deferment periods and greater use of electronic sealing and end‑to‑end digital tracking of consignments.  
- Easing of timelines and conditions for re‑export and export‑related processes for certain sectors (for example, seafood and leather), to support export competitiveness.  

These changes are framed as part of a broader customs‑modernisation and logistics‑efficiency push.


### Digitalisation and Tax Administration


On the administrative side, the budget continues the trend towards digital, rule‑based processes:

- Integration of multiple customs and tax processes into unified IT systems to reduce manual interfaces and delays.  
- Greater use of pre‑filled information, standardised formats, and electronic submission for tax‑related documentation.  
- Closer alignment between accounting standards, Income Computation and Disclosure Standards (ICDS), and tax reporting through a joint committee of the Ministry of Corporate Affairs and CBDT.  

The stated aim is to increase predictability, reduce compliance costs, and support formalisation.


## Conclusion


Union Budget 2026–27 combines a strong public‑investment push with a cautious, clearly articulated fiscal consolidation path.  
Large transport and infrastructure allocations, targeted manufacturing schemes, and people‑centric social investments are backed by an explicit commitment to keep the fiscal deficit and public debt on a downward glide path.  

For policymakers, investors, and practitioners, the central messages are consistent: infrastructure‑led growth, gradual fiscal tightening, deeper domestic manufacturing, and incremental but meaningful reforms in tax administration and trade facilitation.
